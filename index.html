QCD overview
IRA owners age 70½+ can transfer up to $105,000/year (2024, indexed) directly to a qualified charity. Counts toward the RMD but excluded from taxable income — reducing MAGI, SS taxation, and IRMAA exposure.
Tax savings estimated at 22% marginal rate. DAFs do not qualify. Must be a direct IRA-to-charity transfer.
Key QCD rules
— Direct transfer from IRA to charity only (no 60-day rollover)
— Satisfies RMD obligation dollar-for-dollar up to annual limit
— Cannot also claim charitable deduction for same amount
— Reduces AGI / MAGI — prevents SS cliff and IRMAA surcharges
— Not available from 401(k) — IRA only (rollover first if needed)
— Donor-advised funds do NOT qualify as QCD recipients

The pre-65 retirement trap — most common Medicare mistake
A client retires at 62, enrolls in ACA marketplace coverage, and assumes Medicare will notify them at 65. It won't. Unless they are receiving Social Security, Medicare does not auto-enroll them. They must actively sign up during their Initial Enrollment Period. Missing it triggers permanent lifetime penalties on Part B and Part D. One of the most costly and irreversible mistakes in retirement planning.

General Enrollment Period (GEP) — if IEP was missed
If a client misses their IEP and has no SEP, they must wait for the GEP. Every 12-month period without coverage generates a permanent 10% Part B penalty.

ACA plan cancellation — critical steps
First-year Medicare MAGI — ACA subsidy reconciliation risk
In the year of Medicare enrollment, a client may have received ACA premium tax credits for the months they were on the marketplace plan, then switch to Medicare mid-year. The IRS reconciles subsidies based on full-year income. If full-year MAGI is higher than projected (due to Roth conversions, RMDs, or asset sales), the client may owe back some or all of the premium credits received during the ACA months. Flag this in the tax projection for the transition year.
The ACA → Medicare auto-renewal trap — a mirror of the Medicare enrollment trap
A client turns 65 while on an ACA marketplace plan. The ACA plan auto-renews for the next year. Meanwhile, Medicare does not auto-enroll them unless they are already receiving Social Security. The result: the client is paying for ACA marketplace coverage they can no longer receive subsidies for, while accumulating Part B and Part D late enrollment penalties. This is the ACA-side mirror of the Medicare enrollment trap — and it is increasingly common as more early retirees use the marketplace bridge.
The trap mechanism — step by step
Subsidy eligibility ends at 65
Once a client is eligible for Medicare (age 65 or earlier if on SSDI), they are no longer eligible for ACA premium tax credits — even if they remain enrolled in a marketplace plan. If they received subsidies in advance during months when they were Medicare-eligible, they must repay them in full when filing taxes. There is no cap on repayment for clients who are Medicare-eligible.
What the client thinks
My marketplace plan auto-renewed. I have coverage. I'll figure out Medicare later.
What is actually happening
All subsidies received after age 65 are repayable. Part B and D penalty months are accumulating. IEP window may be closing or closed.

Sequence of Returns Risk
Interactive simulator — compare traditional withdrawal vs. bucket model side-by-side. Adjust return sequence, withdrawal rate, and bucket allocation to see how timing of losses shapes long-term outcomes.
Why sequence matters
A retiree withdrawing from a portfolio that drops 30% in year 1 loses far more than just 30% — they lock in losses by selling at the bottom to fund living expenses, permanently reducing the base from which future returns compound. Two retirees with identical 20-year average returns can have dramatically different outcomes if one experiences losses early and the other late.

Your bucket model solves this directly: Bucket A (money market) funds distributions for 2–3 years. Bucket G never needs to be touched during a downturn. FIA positions (Buckets B–F) are principal-protected and not subject to market volatility. The result: clients never sell equities at a loss to fund income.

Advisor conversation framing: "The bucket model doesn't eliminate volatility — it eliminates the need to sell during volatility. Because Bucket A is always funded 2–3 years forward, and FIA positions are principal-protected, we never have to liquidate Bucket G when markets are down. That's the structural answer to sequence of returns risk."
The core mechanic
Forced selling at a loss is the mechanism that makes early downturns so destructive. When a portfolio drops 40% and the retiree still needs $50,000/yr, they sell shares at the bottom. Those shares never compound back. A 40% loss requires a 67% gain just to break even — but with fewer shares, that recovery matters less.

Average return doesn't predict outcome. Two portfolios with the same CAGR over 20 years can have wildly different ending balances based solely on when the bad years occurred. This is the core insight behind SOR risk — and why average return assumptions are insufficient for retirement planning.
Withdrawal rate amplifies the effect
The 4% rule was derived from historical worst-case sequences (1966 retiree) — it does not guarantee success for all future sequences. Higher withdrawal rates amplify SOR risk dramatically.

Dollar withdrawals vs. percentage: When a client takes a fixed dollar amount (e.g., $50k/yr), a portfolio decline means they're selling a larger percentage of the remaining balance. A 40% drop that turns $1M into $600K means $50K is now 8.3% of the portfolio — not 5%. The portfolio mathematically cannot recover.
Why "stay the course" doesn't work in retirement
During accumulation, a market decline is an opportunity — you're buying more shares at lower prices. The math works in your favor.

During distribution, the math reverses. Every withdrawal during a decline is a permanent reduction in base — you are selling shares at the worst time and cannot buy them back. "Staying the course" doesn't repair the damage because the compounding base is permanently smaller.
Structural mitigations
1
Cash buffer (Bucket A): Eliminates forced selling for 2–3 years. Distributions never come from equities.
2
FIA ladder (Buckets B–F): Principal-protected contracts that mature at known dates. Not subject to market drawdowns — they refill Bucket A on schedule.
3
Bucket G as long-term engine: Equity grows untouched during downturns. Never liquidated during a bear market. Only harvested when conditions are favorable.
4
Flexible withdrawal rate: If Bucket G is down materially, defer discretionary spending and extend the buffer before refilling from G.
Common client objections and responses
ObjectionAdvisor response
"The market always comes back."It often does — but your portfolio doesn't come back the same way. If you sold 10% of your shares at the bottom to fund income, those shares never recover. The portfolio is permanently smaller.
"My average return will be 7%."Average return is a planning fiction in retirement. The sequence of those returns determines whether you run out of money. Two identical 7% averages can produce wildly different outcomes.
"Can't I just hold a balanced fund?"A balanced fund is better than pure equity, but you still face the same forced-selling problem during any drawdown. The bucket structure completely separates the income engine from the growth engine.
"Aren't FIAs expensive?"The cost comparison should be: what is the cost of NOT having principal protection during year 1 or 2 of retirement? If a crash happens in year 2, the FIA premium buys you the ability to let Bucket G recover without selling.
