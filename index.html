QCD overview
IRA owners age 70½+ can transfer up to $105,000/year (2024, indexed) directly to a qualified charity. Counts toward the RMD but excluded from taxable income — reducing MAGI, SS taxation, and IRMAA exposure.
Tax savings estimated at 22% marginal rate. DAFs do not qualify. Must be a direct IRA-to-charity transfer.
Key QCD rules
— Direct transfer from IRA to charity only (no 60-day rollover)
— Satisfies RMD obligation dollar-for-dollar up to annual limit
— Cannot also claim charitable deduction for same amount
— Reduces AGI / MAGI — prevents SS cliff and IRMAA surcharges
— Not available from 401(k) — IRA only (rollover first if needed)
— Donor-advised funds do NOT qualify as QCD recipients

The tax torpedo — and how conversions prevent it
The "tax torpedo" occurs when RMDs begin at 73–75 and combine with Social Security income, pushing a retiree into a significantly higher bracket. SS becomes 85% taxable, IRMAA surcharges activate, and the bracket impact is worse than if conversions had been done earlier at lower rates.

Integration with your bucket model
Tax to fund conversions should come from taxable accounts or Bucket A (money market) — not from the IRA itself. Conversions build the Roth IRA as a parallel long-growth position alongside Bucket G, providing tax-free income optionality after age 59½ and a superior legacy asset (no RMDs, tax-free to heirs).

The pre-65 retirement trap — most common Medicare mistake
A client retires at 62, enrolls in ACA marketplace coverage, and assumes Medicare will notify them at 65. It won't. Unless they are receiving Social Security, Medicare does not auto-enroll them. They must actively sign up during their Initial Enrollment Period. Missing it triggers permanent lifetime penalties on Part B and Part D. One of the most costly and irreversible mistakes in retirement planning.

General Enrollment Period (GEP) — if IEP was missed
If a client misses their IEP and has no SEP, they must wait for the GEP. Every 12-month period without coverage generates a permanent 10% Part B penalty.

Permanent lifetime surcharge
Late enrollment penalties are permanent and compound with inflation. They are recalculated annually on the current standard premium — so they grow every time premiums increase. A client 2 years late on Part B will pay 20% more than the standard premium every month for the rest of their life.

Key IRMAA planning rules
— Based on MAGI from two years prior — plan now for premiums two years out
— Affects both spouses on Medicare separately — surcharge is per person
— Roth conversions, capital gains, RMDs, and muni interest all count toward MAGI
— Roth IRA distributions are NOT included in MAGI — the premier long-term defense
— QCDs reduce AGI and MAGI — highly effective for charitable clients age 70½+
— One dollar over a tier threshold triggers the full surcharge (hard cliff effect)
— Part D surcharges are additional (~$13–$84/mo per person by tier)
Pre-65 retirement — Medicare planning checklist
Confirm client's 65th birthday and calculate IEP window (3 months before through 3 months after)
Verify whether client will receive SS before 65 — if yes, confirm auto-enrollment in Medicare Parts A & B
Confirm current health coverage (ACA, COBRA, employer) and whether it is creditable for Part D
Educate client: ACA marketplace plans are NOT creditable for Part D — months accumulate toward penalty
Review MAGI from two years prior — determine IRMAA tier at Medicare enrollment
If IRMAA applies due to a qualifying life event (retirement), prepare SSA-44 appeal
Present Medigap vs. Medicare Advantage decision — emphasize the guaranteed issue window at 65
If choosing Medigap: compare Plan G (no copays) vs. Plan N (lower premium with copays)
Set calendar reminder 4 months before client's 65th birthday — IEP action date
Project IRMAA exposure for next 5 years — coordinate with Roth conversion ladder
Confirm Part D plan enrollment — even if no current prescriptions (avoids future penalty)
During Annual Enrollment Period (Oct 15–Dec 7): review Part D plan for formulary changes

For clients retiring before age 65, the ACA bridge years can create both opportunity and risk. The planner’s job is to coordinate health coverage cost with tax strategy, not treat them separately.

Sequence of Returns Risk
Why early-retirement market declines are uniquely damaging — and how your bucket model addresses this.
Full module coming — interactive SOR simulator comparing traditional withdrawal vs. bucket approach.
Why sequence matters
A retiree withdrawing from a portfolio that drops 30% in year 1 loses far more than just 30% — they lock in losses by selling at the bottom to fund living expenses, permanently reducing the base from which future returns compound. Two retirees with identical 20-year average returns can have dramatically different outcomes if one experiences losses early and the other late.

Your bucket model solves this directly: Bucket A (money market) funds distributions for 2–3 years. Bucket G never needs to be touched during a downturn. FIA positions (Buckets B–F) are principal-protected and not subject to market volatility. The result: clients never sell equities at a loss to fund income.
